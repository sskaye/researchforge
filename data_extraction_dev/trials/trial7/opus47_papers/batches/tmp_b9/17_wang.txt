                OPEN             Preparation and thermal properties
                                 study of HMX/RDX composites
                                 Yu-ting Tao, Shaohua Jin, Lijie Li, Xinya Chen, Kun Chen & Junfeng Wang
                                 HMX/RDX composites were prepared using the solvent-antisolvent alternating method. The
                                 morphology and structure of the composites were characterized by optical microscopy, high-
                                 performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR), and
                                 powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD). The composite exhibited a morphology with HMX as the core and
                                 RDX as the coating layer. The HMX existed in the β-phase, and molecular interactions were observed
                                 between the two components (a blue shift of 2.06 cm− 1 in the NO2 symmetric stretching vibration
                                 peak of HMX and a red shift of 2.06 cm− 1 in the C-H stretching vibration peak of RDX). Thermal
                                 analysis revealed synergistic effects in the thermal decomposition process (lower decomposition
                                 peak temperature for HMX and higher for RDX), with an apparent activation energy (149.41 kJ/mol)
                                 in the first decomposition stage lower than that of the physical mixture and pure RDX component.
                                 Concurrently, both the critical thermal detonation temperature (227.74 ℃) and self-accelerating
                                 decomposition temperature (213.78 ℃) exceed those of the physical mixture and pure RDX
                                 component, thereby demonstrating superior thermal safety. This composite material strikes a balance
                                 between energy release efficiency and safety, offering significant application value for weapon systems
                                 that pursue higher performance and lower vulnerability. It also provides a novel approach and technical
                                 pathway for high-energy explosive formulation design.

                                 Keywords HMX/RDX composites, Solvent-antisolvent alternating method, Thermal decomposition kinetics,
                                 Synergistic effect, Thermal stability

                                 Octahydro-1,3,5,7-tetranitro-1,3,5,7-tetrazocine (HMX) and hexahydro-1,3,5-trinitro-1,3,5-triazine (RDX),
                                 as two representative monocomponent explosives, exhibit energy characteristics and thermal stability that
                                 directly determine the destructive efficiency and safety of weapon systems1–4. HMX demonstrates an explosive
                                 heat of 5674 kJ/kg and a detonation velocity of 9109 m/s5. The substance under discussion is characterized by
                                 high energy characteristics, substantial density, and excellent thermal stability. Consequently, it serves as the
                                 primary energy source in numerous practical propellant formulations6–8. For example, the LX-14 and PBX9404
                                 explosives, as well as the Russian Okfir explosives, are HMX-based PBXs2,9,10. RDX, a classical single-substance
                                 explosive, exhibits a detonation velocity (approximately 8750 m/s) and detonation pressure (approximately 34
                                 GPa) that are slightly lower than HMX11. However, its detonation heat value is comparable to HMX, and its raw
                                 material costs are significantly reduced12. Consequently, it is widely used across various military applications13.
                                 For example, the CH-6 and PBX9407 explosives of the United States are RDX-based PBXs. It is important
                                 to note that, despite HMX demonstrating significantly higher detonation velocity, detonation pressure, and
                                 detonation heat compared to RDX, along with superior thermal stability, its high production cost limits its
                                 widespread application. This trade-off between performance and cost makes the HMX/RDX composite system
                                 a key technological approach for balancing energy output, safety, and economic viability. Through precise
                                 regulation of their mixing ratio, a dynamic balance can be achieved between energy output and safety, satisfying
                                 the requirements of different application scenarios for explosive performance13.
                                     However, the preponderance of extant composite systems is characterized by mechanical mixing
                                 methodologies14. The presence of weak interfacial bonding between the two components, in conjunction with
                                 high crystal defect densities, frequently results in inhomogeneous distribution of components, inadequate mass
                                 uniformity, incomplete detonation reactions, and diminished energy release efficiency. These limitations render
                                 them incapable of meeting the dual requirements of contemporary weapon systems for high energy density and
                                 low sensitivity. This performance bottleneck is in direct conflict with the demand for advanced ‘high-energy
                                 and insensitive’ integrated materials in next-generation armament systems and is becoming a critical factor
                                 restricting their development. Consequently, the exploration of innovative preparation technologies for HMX/
                                 RDX composites has emerged as a research focus within the domain of energetic materials.

                                     Current research on HMX/RDX composite systems primarily focuses on their thermodynamic and kinetic
                                 behaviors: Myint et al. established an equation of state applicable to pure HMX and mixed systems15. McKenney
                                 et al. experimentally constructed the RDX/HMX binary phase diagram, revealing the dominant role of δ-HMX
                                 in thermal decomposition16. Studies by Li Sha et al. demonstrated that the HMX90/RDX10 mixture exhibits
                                 the highest initial decomposition peak temperature (268.12 °C) and critical thermal explosion temperature
                                 (277.66 °C)17. However, Sinapour et al.18 and Xu Yabei et al.19 found that HMX/RDX interactions reduce the
                                 thermal stability of the system-both decomposition temperatures and activation energies at varying conversion
                                 degrees slightly decrease. For instance, when the RDX/HMX ratio decreases from 9:1 to 5:5, the activation
                                 energy of RDX drops from 162.32 kJ/mol to 132.93 kJ/mol. Lu Na et al. investigated the thermal decomposition
                                 pathways of HMX/RDX mixtures, demonstrating that HMX retains its denitration and C-N bond cleavage
                                 mechanisms, while intermolecular oxygen transfer, polymerization, and dimerization occur between HMX and
                                 RDX molecules20. Liu Haiying et al. prepared nano-HMX/micro-RDX composites with varying ratios under
                                 ultrasonic conditions and conducted a comparative analysis of their impact sensitivity with that of pure RDX.
                                 The research demonstrated that the nano-HMX/micro-RDX composite explosives exhibited higher impact
                                 sensitivity compared to pure RDX explosives. However, the impact sensitivity gradually decreased with increasing
                                 HMX content21. Although these studies elucidate the correlation between component ratios and performance,
                                 current preparation methods still fail to address structural defects in the materials. Therefore, selecting effective
                                 composite strategies to optimize HMX/RDX combinations and fabricate HMX/RDX composite energetic
                                 materials with uniform particle size and superior performance remains a critical challenge requiring further
                                 in-depth exploration.
                                     Therefore, this study focuses on the preparation process and thermal properties of β-HMX/RDX composite
                                 materials, aiming to overcome the technical limitations of traditional mechanical mixing methods and establish
                                 a novel β-HMX/RDX composite system to provide foundational data for its applications. In this research, β-
                                 HMX/RDX composites with a mass ratio of 4:6 were prepared using a solvent-antisolvent alternating method.
                                 The morphology, structural composition, component distribution, and surface chemical state of the composites
                                 were systematically characterized by optical microscopy, X-ray diffraction (XRD), high-performance liquid
                                 chromatography (HPLC), and Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR). Furthermore, the thermal
                                 performance of the composites was comprehensively evaluated through thermal analysis.

                                 Materials and methods
                                 Materials
                                 Raw HMX and raw RDX were synthesized in the laboratory and purified via a recrystallization method, achieving
                                 a mass fraction purity exceeding 99%. Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, analytical grade) was supplied by Shanghai
                                 Macklin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd. Ultrapure water was provided by Fisher Chemical. Acetonitrile
                                 (HPLC grade) was obtained from Beijing Tongguang Fine Chemicals Company, and methanol (HPLC grade)
                                 was sourced from Ron Reagent.

                                 Preparation of HMX/RDX composites
                                 Following a comprehensive analysis of the solubility data for HMX and RDX, it was determined that 80%
                                 dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) would serve as the optimal solvent. Ultrapure water was then selected as the
                                 antisolvent medium for the recrystallisation experiment, employing a solvent-antisolvent alternating method.
                                 Under a thermostatic water bath maintained at 25 ℃, HMX and RDX in a mass ratio of 4:6 were added to an
                                 appropriate amount of 80% DMSO solvent and stirred until fully dissolved. The solution was then dripped via
                                 a syringe pump into ultrapure water (37.5% of the solution volume) at a controlled rate of 0.6 mL/min under
                                 stirring at 140 rpm, resulting in the precipitation of white crystals. After completion of the dripping process, the
                                 mixture was stirred at 25 ℃ for 90 min. Subsequently, ultrapure water was introduced into the suspension at a
                                 controlled rate of 0.6 mL/min. The system temperature was then elevated to 55 ℃, held for 3 h, and subsequently
                                 cooled to 25 ℃. The final HMX/RDX composites were isolated via sequential filtration, washing, and drying.

                                 Structural characterization of HMX/RDX composites
                                 Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) was performed on a D/teX Ultra 250 diffractometer (Smartlab SE, Tokyo, Japan)
                                 in reflection mode under operating conditions of 40 kV and 50 mA, employing Cu-Kβ radiation with a step
                                 size of 0.01° and a scanning range of 5°-90°. Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) was conducted
                                 using a Bruker ALPHA II spectrometer to characterize the crystal structure of the HMX/RDX composites, with
                                 a spectral range of 4000–400 cm− 1 and 64 scans. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC, LC-100
                                 model, Shanghai Wufeng Scientific Instruments Co., Ltd.) was utilized to assess the purity of components in the
                                 composites. The HPLC analysis employed a stainless-steel column (ODS stationary phase, 5 μm particle size,
                                 4.6 nm internal diameter, 20 cm length) with a mobile phase of acetonitrile/methanol/water (v/v/v), an injection
                                 volume of 10 µL, and a detection wavelength of 230 nm.
                                     The thermal properties of pure α-HMX, β-HMX, and the β-HMX/RDX composites were analyzed using a
                                 Labsys Evo STA synchronous thermal analyzer (SETARAM, Lyon, France). The samples were heated from 50 ℃
                                 to 400 ℃ at a heating rate of 10 ℃/min in aluminum oxide (Al2O3) crucibles. Thermogravimetry-differential
                                 scanning calorimetry (TG-DSC) was further conducted on raw β-HMX, RDX, and the β-HMX/RDX composites
                                 under a nitrogen atmosphere, with a temperature ramp from 50 °C to 400 °C at 10 ℃/min.

                                 FT-IR
                                 Figure 3 displays the infrared spectra of HMX, RDX, and the HMX/RDX composites. Typically, β-HMX exhibits
                                 no distinct peaks in the wavenumber range of 700–740 cm− 122. As seen in Fig. 3, the composite material displays
                                 characteristic absorption peaks specific to β-HMX (3035.50 cm− 1 and 1139.34 cm− 1), while the characteristic
                                 peaks of α-HMX in the 700–800 cm− 1 range are absent. Therefore, it can be concluded that HMX in the
                                 composite primarily exists in the β-phase. Hence, “HMX” in subsequent sections specifically refers to β-HMX.
                                 Furthermore, the composite’s infrared spectrum simultaneously exhibits characteristic infrared peaks from
                                 both HMX and RDX: the C-N bending vibration peak of RDX at 672.50 cm− 1 and the C-N bending vibration
                                 peak of HMX at 656.05 cm− 1; the RDX -NO2 stretching vibration peak at 779.38 cm− 1 and the HMX -NO2
                                 vibration peak at 752.71 cm− 1. It is noteworthy that the peaks at 672.50 cm− 1, 656.05 cm− 1, and 779.38 cm− 1
                                 demonstrate a 2.06 cm− 1 blue shift relative to their corresponding infrared peaks in pure components. This
                                 finding suggests that intermolecular interactions between HMX and RDX result in alterations to their chemical
                                 environment. Furthermore, characteristic peaks for HMX are observed at 1085.87 cm− 1 and 1139.34 cm− 1 in
                                 the composite’s infrared spectrum, with the 1139.34 cm− 1 peak displaying a 2.06 cm− 1 blue shift relative to pure
                                 HMX. Furthermore, the –NO2 symmetric stretching vibration peak of RDX at 1307.90 cm− 1 exhibited a blue
                                 shift of 2.10 cm− 1 relative to its pure-phase peak position. Furthermore, the C-H stretching vibration peak at
                                 3068.41 cm− 1, attributed to RDX, exhibited an infrared shift of 2.06 cm− 1 in comparison to pure RDX.
                                     The existence of these distinctive peaks attests to the retention of HMX and RDX’s original chemical structures
                                 within the composites. In the meantime, the chromatographic peaks’ blue and red shifts show that HMX and
                                 RDX’s intermolecular interactions have changed the molecules’ vibrational frequencies.

                                 PXRD
                                 The XRD patterns of raw HMX, raw RDX, and HMX/RDX composites are displayed in Fig. 4. As demonstrated
                                 in the figure, the characteristic diffraction peaks corresponding to HMX and RDX are distinctly observed in the
                                 PXRD patterns of the HMX/RDX composites. Specifically, the peaks at 2θ = 13.01° and 25.26° are assigned to
                                 RDX, while those at 2θ = 14.65°, 26.12°, and 31.88° correspond to HMX. Notably, certain peaks in the composite
                                 diffraction pattern demonstrate shifts in comparison to the individual peaks of HMX and RDX components,
                                 yet no novel diffraction peaks were identified. This phenomenon is indicative of significant intermolecular
                                 interactions within the HMX/RDX composite system. Such interactions may induce subtle modifications to the
                                 crystal lattice structure, leading to peak displacement. Furthermore, the absence of discernible crystalline phases
                                 during the composite preparation process is indicated.

                                 Thermal decomposition behavior and kinetics
                                 The TG curves of HMX, RDX, and HMX/RDX composites are shown in Fig. 5. The TG-DTG curve obtained at
                                 a heating rate of 10 ℃/min clearly shows that the thermal decomposition process of the HMX/RDX composite
                                 occurs in two different stages with overlapping decomposition peaks. The first decomposition stage begins at
                                 191.06 ℃ and reaches its maximum rate at 246.18 ℃, corresponding to the thermal decomposition of RDX
                                 (Fig. 5b). The second stage has been shown to reach its maximum decomposition rate at 266.50 ℃, which
                                 corresponds to the decomposition of HMX (Fig. 5a). The process of decomposition is complete at 281.44 ℃.
                                    These results demonstrate that the intermolecular interactions between HMX and RDX in the composite
                                 induce earlier initiation of HMX decomposition at a reduced temperature. Furthermore, the thermal
                                 decomposition of RDX accelerates the HMX decomposition reaction, revealing a mutual synergistic effect
                                 between the two components.

                                 Calculation
                                 This study successfully synthesised HMX/RDX composite materials with a core-shell-like structure using the
                                 alternating solvent-antisolvent method. The key findings are as follows:

                                 1. The composite has a uniform structure, with HMX forming the core and RDX forming the coating layer.
                                    FT-IR and XRD results confirm the β-crystalline form of HMX within the composite and reveal molecular
                                    interactions between the two components. Concurrently, HPLC analysis indicates precise control over the
                                    HMX-to-RDX ratio within the composite, yielding high-purity products free from impurity interference.
                                 2. It has been established that a two-stage thermal decomposition reaction occurs in the composite material. A
                                    comparison of the peak decomposition temperatures attributable to HMX and RDX reveals a decrease in the
                                    former and an increase in the latter. This finding suggests the presence of a substantial thermal synergistic
                                    effect between the two components. The heat released from RDX decomposition accelerates the subsequent
                                    decomposition process of the HMX component, thereby enhancing the overall thermal responsiveness of the
                                    composite. Kissinger kinetic analysis confirms the enhanced detonation activity of the composite. The ap-
                                    parent activation energy (149.41 kJ/mol) during the first thermal decomposition stage is significantly lower
                                    than that of the physical mixture of the same components and pure RDX. It is evident that the critical thermal
                                    detonation temperature (227.74 ℃) and the self-accelerating decomposition temperature of this composite
                                    are higher than those of the physical mixture system of the same components, indicating superior thermal
                                    safety.

                                 The HMX/RDX composites leverage synergistic effects of their components and a core-shell structure design to
                                 enhance thermal breakdown performance while ensuring safety. Their excellent thermal stability guarantees safe
                                 storage, and their low activation energy renders them attractive for applications requiring rapid energy release
                                 (e.g. propellants or starting charges). By controlling the composite ratio and the crystallization process, it may
                                 be possible to further optimize energy output and stability matching in the future.
